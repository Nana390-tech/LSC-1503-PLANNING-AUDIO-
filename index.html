🎙️ Audio Presentation Plan

Record a short audio overview of your TED Talk plan — your big idea, your story, and your slides — before your final exam

🎤 Teams Assignment
⭐ 5% of Grade
💡 TED Talk — Plan Stage
🎙️

What is this activity?

Before your final TED Talk presentation, you will record a short audio overview of your plan on Microsoft Teams. This is not your full presentation — it is a planning summary that shows your teacher you are organised, prepared, and thinking deeply about your talk.

What kind of topic is a TED Talk topic?

Your TED Talk is about a big idea — something you believe, something that changed how you think, or something that could change how others see the world. It is personal, human, and idea-driven. It is not a career report or a school essay. For example:

💛 Kindness is contagious
😴 Why sleep changes everything
❌ The power of saying no
🌱 How failure helps us grow
📵 What I learned from quitting social media
🤝 Listening is a superpower
🧠 The lies we tell ourselves
⏳ Stop waiting for the right moment

Your topic should be one clear idea that your audience can feel something about, think differently about, and remember long after your talk ends.

💡

This is a PLAN, not a performance

You do not need to deliver your full talk in this recording. Just explain your thinking — what your big idea is, how you plan to open, what personal story you will tell, how your argument is built, and what your slides look like. Think of it as telling a friend: "This is what my TED Talk is going to be about and why."

📋

What to cover in your audio

Your 3–5 minute recording should touch on all five parts below.

1

💡 Your Big Idea

State your TED Talk topic and its core message. What do you want your audience to think, feel, or do differently after hearing you?

2

🪝 Your Hook

How will you open your talk in the first 30 seconds? A question, a surprising fact, a bold statement, or a vivid moment from your own life?

3

📖 Your Personal Story

What real experience connects you to this idea? The best TED Talks are anchored in honest, human moments — not just facts and opinions.

4

📌 Your Supporting Points

What 2–3 ideas, examples, or pieces of evidence will you use to convince your audience that your big idea is true and important?

5

🎨 Your Slide Design

Describe your PowerPoint choices — colours, images, fonts, layout — and explain WHY those choices suit the tone and mood of your topic.

🌟

Why does this matter?

This recording counts as 5% of your overall grade. More importantly, saying your plan out loud before the final exam helps you discover gaps and strengthen your argument while there is still time to improve. Your teacher can also give you personalised feedback based on this recording.

Your Audio Script Guide

Use these examples and sentence starters as a guide — you do NOT need to copy them. Speak naturally from your own notes. All examples below use the idea "Kindness is contagious" so you can see how the structure works in practice.

⏱️

Target: 3–5 minutes total

Spend roughly 30–60 seconds on each section. Do not read from a written script — it will sound flat and unnatural. Use bullet-point notes and speak from what you genuinely know and feel about your topic.

1
Opening — Your name, topic, and core message
Begin by greeting your teacher, naming your TED Talk, and stating your core message in one clear sentence.
"Hi, my name is [name], and I am in LSC 1503. My TED Talk is called Kindness is Contagious. My core message is simple: one small act of kindness can spread further than you ever expect — and understanding that can genuinely change the way you move through the world."
💬 Two things your listener needs right away: your topic title and your one-sentence message — what you want the audience to leave believing.
"In this recording I will walk you through my plan — my hook, my personal story, my key supporting points, and my slide design."
💬 A short roadmap at the start helps your listener follow your plan easily.
My TED Talk is called…
My big idea is…
My core message is…
I want my audience to believe that…
2
Part 1 — My Hook 🪝
Describe how you plan to open your TED Talk in the first 30 seconds. Name the hook type you are using and explain WHY it suits your idea.
"To open my talk, I am going to ask the audience a question: 'When was the last time a stranger was kind to you — and how did it make you feel for the rest of that day?' I chose a question hook because it immediately makes the audience look inward and connect the topic to their own lived experience, before I have even made a single argument."
💬 Hook types: a rhetorical question, a surprising statistic, a bold statement, a vivid personal moment, or a quote. Always say WHY this hook fits your particular idea.
"I considered opening with a statistic — that witnessing an act of kindness releases oxytocin in the observer, not just the receiver — but I decided the question felt more personal and less like a lecture."
💬 Explaining a choice you decided AGAINST shows genuine critical thinking.
My hook is…
I plan to open with…
I chose this hook because…
I want the audience to feel…
3
Part 2 — My Personal Story 📖
Briefly describe the real personal experience that connects YOU to this idea. You do not need to tell the full story here — just describe it and explain how it links to your message.
"After the hook, I will share a story from when I was 14. I was having a really difficult week at school, and a girl I barely knew left a kind note on my desk. I did not know her well, but that small thing stayed with me for years. In my talk, I will use this story to show that kindness does not need to be planned or big — it just needs to be real."
💬 Real TED Talks always anchor the argument in a human moment. Your story does not need to be dramatic — it just needs to be true and clearly connected to your idea.
"I chose this story because it shows the idea rather than just stating it. I want the audience to see that my topic is not theoretical — it comes from something that actually happened."
My story is about a time when…
This experience taught me that…
I chose this story because…
This connects to my idea by…
4
Part 3 — My Supporting Points 📌
Describe the 2–3 ideas, examples, or evidence you will use to support and develop your big idea. These are not separate sub-topics — they are reasons or proof that your idea is true.
"To support my idea that kindness is contagious, I have three points. First, the science — neuroscience research shows that watching someone be kind can produce what researchers call 'moral elevation' in the observer — a warm, motivating feeling that makes them more likely to be kind themselves. Second, a real-world example — I will talk about the 'caffè sospeso' tradition in Italy, where customers pay for an extra coffee for a stranger they will never meet. Third, a personal and cultural connection — the Islamic concept of sadaqah, and how everyday generosity is woven into the way many of us were raised here in the UAE."
💬 Strong TED Talks often mix science, real-world examples, and personal or cultural relevance. Make sure each point actually proves your idea — not just describes something loosely related to it.
My first point is…
To support my idea I will…
A real example of this is…
This proves my idea because…
5
Part 4 — My Slide Design 🎨
This section is especially important for your oral defence. Explain your visual choices — colour, font, image style, amount of text — and where you sourced your images. Be ready to justify every design decision.
"For my slides, I chose a warm palette — cream and terracotta tones — because kindness feels warm, human, and gentle, not cold or corporate. I kept each slide very minimal: usually one image and just a few words, because I want the audience to listen to me, not read a screen full of bullet points."
"For my images, I used Unsplash — it is free and copyright-safe. I searched for real, candid photographs of human connection — people helping each other, quiet moments of care — rather than posed stock photos that look artificial. I think using genuine, natural images better supports a talk about authentic kindness."
💬 Your teacher will ask where you got your images in the oral defence. Always name the source — Unsplash, Pexels, Canva, or your own photos — and explain why you chose those specific images, not just any images.
"I chose a simple, readable sans-serif font because I did not want the typography to compete with the emotional weight of the content. The design should serve the message, not distract from it."
I chose these colours because…
My slides are minimal because…
I sourced my images from…
My font choice was… because…
The design matches my topic because…
6
Part 5 — My Preparation & Closing 🗣️
End by briefly explaining how you are preparing to deliver your TED Talk — and close politely and professionally.
"To prepare my delivery, I am practising out loud every day and timing myself to make sure I stay within the limit. I also recorded myself on my phone once and listened back — it helped me catch my filler words and hear whether my pacing was natural."
"One thing I am still working on is reducing filler words like 'um' and 'like', and making more eye contact instead of looking at my notes. I know that looking at the audience is what makes a TED Talk feel personal rather than like a report being read aloud."
"That is the end of my presentation plan. Thank you for listening, and I look forward to your feedback."
💬 A professional, polite close matters — this is a spoken communication course. Always thank the listener.
To prepare I am…
I practise by…
One thing I am still working on is…
Thank you for listening.

Recording Tips

Find a quiet room. Speak at a calm, natural pace — silence is not a mistake. Do NOT read word-for-word from a written script; it will sound flat. Use bullet-point notes and speak from what you genuinely think and feel. You can re-record as many times as you need before submitting.

How to Submit on Microsoft Teams

Your teacher has created an Assignment for you on Teams. Follow these steps to record and submit your audio plan.

Step-by-Step Guide
1

Open Microsoft Teams

Log in with your HCT student account and go to your LSC 1503 class channel.

2

Find the Assignment

Click Assignments in the top menu. Look for the assignment named "Audio Presentation Plan" or as named by your teacher.

3

Record Your Audio

Click Add work → Video / Audio recording. Teams will open a recorder. Allow microphone access when prompted.

4

Make Your Recording

Cover the five sections: Big Idea → Hook → Personal Story → Supporting Points → Slide Design. Aim for 3–5 minutes. Re-record if you need to.

5

Review & Attach

Play your recording back once to check audio quality is clear. Attach it to the assignment. You may also include any planning notes you used.

6

Submit ✅

Click Turn in and confirm. Do this before the deadline your teacher has set.

🎧

Audio quality tips

Use earphones with a built-in microphone for clearer sound. Record in a quiet space — a bedroom or study room works well. Avoid fans, open windows, or echo-heavy rooms. Speak close to the microphone but not directly into it.

Alternative: Record on your phone first

If you prefer to record outside Teams first, that is fine too.

A

Record on your phone

Use Voice Memos (iPhone) or Voice Recorder / Sound Recorder (Android).

B

Save the file

Export as .m4a or .mp3 — both are accepted in Teams.

C

Upload to Teams

In your assignment, click Add work → Upload from device → select your file → Turn in.

⚠️

Important reminders

Submit before the deadline. Make sure your name is clearly stated at the start of your recording. If you have a technical issue, contact your teacher as early as possible — do not wait until the last minute.
